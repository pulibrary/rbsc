Introduction

Tweet

In the late eighteenth century, new methods of printing made publishing illustrated books much easier. Color illustrations no longer had to be printed in monochrome and colored by hand, which meant that publishers were able to produce large illustrated books that were intended for viewing, not reading.

Most of these books were published in London, but the artists whose drawings and paintings illustrated them traveled all over the world. Some of them made long and occasionally dangerous trips to sketch their subjects, sometimes giving the public its first view of an unfamiliar landscape.

Color plate books appealed to both those who wanted to commemorate their years abroad and those who wanted a view of exotic landscapes they would never visit. Books of domestic British scenes or newly-acquired colonial territory allowed owners to take pride in their country and its expanding empire. Artists and publishers made an effort to satisfy a public which wanted both absolute accuracy and picturesque scenes.

By the mid-nineteenth century, the market for color plate book began to decline. Photography had replaced the hard work of producing accurate views while revealing the unpleasant parts of colonial life which color plate books did not show.

These illustrations come from British color plate books published between 1775 and 1898 and collected by Leonard L. Milberg, Class of 1953.
